# Composition principles

Distilled from William Strunk Jr., *The Elements of Style* (1918, public domain; full text in [obra/the-elements-of-style](https://github.com/obra/the-elements-of-style) or on Project Gutenberg). Strunk's grammar rules (possessives, comma placement) are omitted because competent writers and models already apply them, along with rulings usage has since overruled (shall/will, the split-infinitive ban, avoiding singular they). What remains is the part that stays true: how to build paragraphs, sentences, and word choices that carry weight.

## Paragraphs

**One topic per paragraph.** The paragraph break is a signal readers rely on; spend it on real shifts of topic, not at random. A new aspect of the subject gets a new paragraph.

**Lead with the topic sentence.** Open with the sentence that states the point, support it in the middle, and end on the sentence you want to stick, usually the consequence. Skimmers read first sentences; make each one the paragraph's promise.

**Rule, then exception, then example.** When defining how something works, state the general rule first, the exceptions second, and a concrete example last. Readers anchor on the first form they meet. (A games-manual convention rather than Strunk, but it generalizes to any procedural writing.)

## Sentences

**Use the active voice.** It is shorter and it names the actor. "My first visit to Boston will always be remembered by me" → "I'll always remember my first visit to Boston." Passive voice earns its place only when the receiver of the action is the true subject ("The suspect was arrested at dawn").

**Put statements in positive form.** Say what a thing is. "Did not remember" → "forgot"; "did not pay attention to" → "ignored"; "not honest" → "dishonest". Reserve "not" for denial and contrast, where it hits hard precisely because you use it rarely.

**Use definite, specific, concrete language.** "A period of unfavorable weather set in" → "It rained every day for a week." The surest way to hold attention is to be specific; generalities let the reader's eye slide.

**Omit needless words.** Strunk's own words are the standard: "Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts." Standard deletions:

- "the question as to whether" → "whether"
- "there is no doubt but that" → "no doubt"
- "used for fuel purposes" → "used for fuel"
- "he is a man who" → "he"
- "in a hasty manner" → "hastily"
- "owing to the fact that" → "since"
- "there is / there are" openers → recast with the real subject

**Avoid a succession of loose sentences.** Paragraph after paragraph of "X, and Y", "X, but Y" turns mechanical. Recast some sentences short and direct, and hold the main clause of others back until the end.

**Express coordinate ideas in similar form.** Parallel content deserves parallel form: "The French, the Italians, Spanish, and Portuguese" → "The French, the Italians, the Spanish, and the Portuguese." The converse also holds: don't force parallel form onto ideas that aren't parallel (see the manufactured triad in ai-tells.md).

**Keep related words together.** Subject near verb; modifier beside the thing it modifies. "He noticed a large stain in the rug that was right in the center" → "He noticed a large stain right in the center of the rug."

**Place emphatic words at the end.** The last position in a sentence carries the stress. "This steel is principally used for making razors, because of its hardness" → "Because of its hardness, this steel is used principally for making razors."

**In summaries, keep one tense.** Summarize a work's contents in the present tense ("the protagonist discovers...") and stay there; drifting between past and present unsettles the reader.

## Word choices that still trip writers

- **fewer / less**: fewer for counted things, less for quantity. Fewer requests, less traffic.
- **affect / effect**: affect is the verb (to influence); effect is the noun (the result).
- **compare to / compare with**: compare to likens; compare with weighs differences.
- **that / which**: prefer "that" for clauses that narrow the meaning; "which" with a comma for asides.
- **very, really, quite**: cut, or choose a stronger word. "Very fast" → "instant".
- **"interesting", "funny" as announcements**: show the thing; don't label it. If it's interesting, the reader will notice.
- **factor, case, nature, aspect**: flab that signals a vague thought. "Unreliable in nature" → "unreliable".
- **however (sentence-opening)**: often smoother mid-sentence ("It failed, however, to...") or replaced with a plain "But".
